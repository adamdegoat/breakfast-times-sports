Sport Science & S&C

Performance insights delivered without mercy

🔬SPORT SCIENCE · NEUROSCIENCE / PHYSIOLOGY

Heavy Squats Make You Faster. Science Says So.

Post-activation potentiation works by phosphorylating myosin regulatory light chains after a heavy loading bout, which means muscle fibres fire faster and harder in the four to twelve minutes that follow — like warming up a turbocharger before flooring it on the motorway. The research is unambiguous: a set of back squats at 85% of one-rep max can acutely increase jump height by five percent and sprint velocity by measurable, reproducible margins. The body, given the right stimulus, temporarily becomes a better version of itself.

On-field application

Front-load warm-ups with a heavy squat or trap bar deadlift, wait eight minutes, then hit sprint or jump work — the conversion from iron to turf is almost embarrassingly direct and costs nothing except slightly rearranging the session plan.

🏋️S&C · POWER

One Lift. Every Explosive Moment in Rugby.

Protocol 5×3 @ 72–78% 1RM, 3 min inter-set rest, performed at the start of the session when the nervous system is fresh and unsullied by fatigue

Pull from the hang position — bar just above the knee — drive through the floor like it personally insulted you, then explode violently through the hips, shrug with genuine intent, and catch the bar in a quarter-squat with elbows high and fast. It is not a bicep curl. The bar rises because the hips drove through; if they did not, the bar will tell you immediately by staying stubbornly earthbound and making everyone feel slightly embarrassed.

Rugby transfer

Triple extension — ankle, knee, hip — is the mechanical signature of every dominant tackle, lineout lift, and scrum drive, and this single lift trains all three simultaneously at match-relevant velocities.

🌙RECOVERY · COLD WATER IMMERSION

Sit in Freezing Water. Hurt Considerably Less Tomorrow.

Protocol 10–12 minutes at 11–14°C within 30 minutes of the final whistle or session end; no wetsuits, no gradual easing in, no negotiating with the temperature — simply get in and breathe through it like a professional

Immersion at 11–15°C drives peripheral vasoconstriction and blunts the prostaglandin-mediated inflammatory cascade that turns Tuesday's legs into structural concrete by Wednesday morning — the mechanism is well-established, the discomfort is, frankly, extraordinary, and anyone who claims to enjoy it is lying. Meta-analyses consistently show reductions in perceived soreness and functional strength loss at 24 and 48 hours post-exercise compared to passive rest.

For rugby

Given that a professional rugby player absorbs approximately 30–40 high-intensity collisions per match, cold water immersion is not a spa indulgence — it is structural maintenance on machinery that will be asked to do exactly the same thing again in six days.

Coaching Corner

Five insights. With pedagogical notes. You're welcome.

⚡ATTACK

Your 10 Is Hiding, Find Him Again

The fly-half who drifts ten metres off the gain line before receiving the ball is not a playmaker — he is a man queuing for a bus that has already left. Get him flat. Get him running onto the pass with his shoulders square and his eyes up, and suddenly the defence has a problem it cannot solve at walking pace. Width is earned by depth first, then by aggression, and any 10 who forgets that should be reminded with the kind of clarity usually reserved for tax demands.

🎓 Coaching Method

Run a 3v2 channel drill with a gate marker forcing the 10 to receive inside it — guided discovery, because apparently telling people things directly went out of fashion sometime around 2015, so let them work it out themselves by running into defenders until the penny drops.

🏉SET-PIECE

A Lineout Without a Caller Is Just Confusion

The lineout caller is the air traffic controller of your set-piece — and right now most amateur teams are running their lineout like Heathrow during a thunderstorm, with everyone guessing and nobody listening. One voice. Numbered calls with a live indicator to shift the code. The jumpers rehearse each lift combination until it is mechanical, which means twice a week, not twice a season. Get this right and you are manufacturing possession from nothing; get it wrong and you are handing the opposition a restart from their own 22.

🎓 Coaching Method

Command style is correct here and anyone who says otherwise has never watched a lineout dissolve in real time — the caller drills the sequence, the team executes, debrief is immediate and forensic.

🛡DEFENCE

Blitz Defence Run Wrong Is Just Sprinting Towards Embarrassment

A blitz line that goes up at different speeds is not a blitz — it is a series of individual men running enthusiastically into their own doom, leaving gaps wide enough to drive a Transit van through. The trigger must be universal: ball leaves the 9's hands, the entire line moves as one thing, like a well-timed contraflow on the M6. The inside shoulder is the target, always, because if the defender hits air on the outside the attacker is already gone and the conversation afterwards is deeply unpleasant.

🎓 Coaching Method

Reciprocal coaching works a treat here — pair defenders up, one watches the partner's trigger timing and calls it, then swap. Clarkson finds the whole concept of peers teaching peers mildly chaotic, but it is genuinely more effective than shouting from the touchline.

💪FITNESS

Rugby Fitness Is Intervals, Not Marathons, Obviously

The player who can run 10km without stopping is almost entirely useless at rugby, which is played in violent four-second bursts followed by thirty seconds of something approaching human recovery. Train the actual energy system: 20-metre accelerations, 40-metre efforts, short rest, repeat until the body learns to produce power when it is already half-dead. Do this twice a week across pre-season and the team that looked shattered by the 60th minute will still be making ground in the 75th.

🎓 Coaching Method

Game-based conditioning — small-sided games with a possession-heavy structure — disguises the fact that players are essentially doing interval training, which is merciful because if you tell a rugby player they are doing intervals they look at you as though you have suggested something criminal.

🧠GAME MANAGEMENT

Stop Kicking to Touch From Your Own 22

Kicking the ball dead from inside your own 22 and then standing around watching the opposition set up a lineout five metres from your try line is one of the great recurring catastrophes of amateur rugby, and it must stop. If the kick must come, it goes long and in-field, forcing the chase and buying the team time to reorganise behind the ball. Carry first, clear second, kick to touch last — and only when there is genuine territory to be gained, not simply because panic has arrived and taken the 9's decision-making with it.

🎓 Coaching Method

Socratic questioning at the review: ask the 9 what options existed before the kick, where the pressure was, what a carry would have produced — let them arrive at the obvious answer themselves, which is less humiliating than being told and, inexplicably, more likely to stick.
