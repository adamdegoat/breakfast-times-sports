Sport Science & S&C

Performance insights delivered without mercy

🔬SPORT SCIENCE · NEUROSCIENCE

Your Muscles Remember. Exploit Them Ruthlessly.

Post-activation potentiation — PAP, because scientists love acronyms — describes the phenomenon whereby a heavy conditioning stimulus, something north of 85% 1RM, temporarily phosphorylates myosin regulatory light chains and sensitises the motor neuron pool, squeezing an additional 3-8% peak power output from subsequent explosive efforts. The effect peaks between 8 and 12 minutes post-stimulus, which is an inconveniently precise window that the body, with characteristic indifference to coach schedules, absolutely refuses to negotiate on. It is, simultaneously, one of the most elegant and most irritating things sport science has ever discovered.

On-field application

A heavy squat or trap bar deadlift set placed 8-12 minutes before sprint or jump work in training sessions is not faff — it is priming the nervous system to produce force at a rate that would otherwise require months of grinding, and every serious rugby programme should be built around it.

🏋️S&C · POWER

The Only Lift That Builds Rugby Power.

Protocol 5×3 @ 75-80% 1RM, 3 min full rest between sets

The bar starts mid-thigh — not the floor, not some vague shin height — because this removes the slack and forces a violent, unambiguous hip extension that is, structurally, identical to a collision, a lineout lift, or the explosive push out of a scrum. The cue is simple: jump and shrug. The hips snap open, the shoulders fire upward as if the ceiling has personally offended them, and the elbows punch through fast enough to catch the bar in a solid front rack. Anything resembling a bicep curl is grounds for immediate ejection from the gym and a long conversation about wasted potential.

Rugby transfer

The triple extension — ankle, knee, hip — firing in sequence is the same pattern that drives a prop through a scrummage, a back-rower off the breakdown, or a winger into a second gear that opposition fullbacks remember for weeks.

🌙RECOVERY · COLD WATER IMMERSION

Sit In The Ice. Stop Complaining. Recover Faster.

Protocol 10-15 minutes at 10-15°C, whole-body immersion to the iliac crest minimum, within 30 minutes of the final whistle or end of training. Not a cool shower. Not 'fairly cold'. Fill the tub, add ice, get in, endure it.

Immersion at 10-15°C drives peripheral vasoconstriction and exploits hydrostatic pressure gradients to flush lactate, cytokines, and inflammatory markers out of skeletal muscle at a rate that passive rest simply cannot match — the tissue is, effectively, being wrung out like a wet cloth, which is both an accurate description and a reasonable preview of how it feels. The blunted inflammatory cascade means legs that would otherwise feel like unset concrete on Thursday after a Wednesday match are instead available for meaningful training, which is the entire point.

For rugby

With a standard 5-7 day turnaround between matches, CWI compresses the inflammatory window aggressively enough to restore neuromuscular function by Tuesday — which is, with grim predictability, exactly when coaches schedule the session that decides selection.

Coaching Corner

Five insights. With pedagogical notes. You're welcome.

⚡ATTACK

Your Flat Line Is Killing Everyone's Joy

A backline that runs flat is not dynamic — it is a queue at a post office, and the defence is the counter staff who knows you haven't got your passport. The first receiver must fix the defensive line before the ball moves; depth creates the angle, the angle creates the overlap, and the overlap creates the try. If your centres are taking contact ten metres behind the gainline, you have not run an attack, you have organised a very expensive tackle.

🎓 Coaching Method

Use a passive-defender grid drill where the channel only opens if the ball-carrier has genuinely committed a defender — guided discovery, because telling them outright feels too much like admitting they should have worked this out by age fourteen.

🏉SET-PIECE

The Scrum Is Physics, Not Aggression

Most amateur scrums collapse not because of weakness but because of angle — props binding too high, hips rising like a frightened cat, the whole unit converting a horizontal force into a vertical disaster. Bind low, lock the core, drive through the heels: the scrum should feel like a well-loaded freight train, not eight men arguing in a cupboard. Get the body position right and suddenly even your osteopath-dependent loosehead becomes a weapon.

🎓 Coaching Method

Command style is unavoidable here — it is biomechanics, not philosophy, and Socratic questioning mid-scrum is a good way to get someone's ear bitten off. Isolate the bind and hip position in pairs before adding the full unit.

🛡DEFENCE

Rushing Up Broken Is Worse Than Standing Still

A defensive line that sprints up without staying connected is not aggressive — it is a motorway contraflow where someone forgot to put cones on the gaps. One man too fast creates a channel; one man too slow creates a mismatch; and the inside centre who does both simultaneously should be told, gently but firmly, to stop. The line speed means nothing if it arrives in pieces — controlled, connected, decisive is the only combination that works.

🎓 Coaching Method

Reciprocal coaching works brilliantly: pair defenders and have one watch the other's drift versus blitz timing, then swap. They will notice the flaw in each other long before they notice it in themselves, which saves the coach having to repeat it seventeen times.

💪FITNESS

Aerobic Base First, Or Nothing Else Matters

Players who skip aerobic conditioning to do repeated sprint work are building a penthouse on a foundation of damp cardboard. The aerobic system is the engine that recovers between high-intensity efforts; without it, your player's repeat sprint quality degrades after twenty minutes and they spend the second half impersonating a confused tourist. Twelve to sixteen weeks of honest zone-two aerobic work before preseason conditioning is not optional — it is the price of admission.

🎓 Coaching Method

Game-based learning suits this better than track sessions that feel like punishment — small-sided games with large spaces and a work-to-rest ratio that enforces continuous movement keep players engaged while quietly building the engine they swore they didn't need.

🧠GAME MANAGEMENT

Stop Kicking Into The Wind At Fullback

Kicking long into a fifteen-knot headwind when your team needs field position is the rugby equivalent of reversing up a motorway — technically possible, catastrophically stupid, and always someone else's fault afterwards. With wind against, kick short and wide to contest, or kick low and chase hard to pin the fullback; kicking high and long into the wind is simply donating possession to a team that is already winning. The gameplan must acknowledge the weather exists, because the weather very much does.

🎓 Coaching Method

Socratic questioning is perfect here: ask the decision-maker what they were trying to achieve, then ask if it worked, then watch the realisation arrive like a slow but inevitable tide. It hurts more when they work it out themselves, which means they remember it longer.
